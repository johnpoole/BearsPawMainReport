DESIGN STANDARDS AT THE TIME OF MANUFACTURE
Since the City of Calgary Bearspaw Water Supply Pipeline plans provided by Pure were dated April
1974, the design would likely have conformed to ASTM A227-71.  According to the AWWA c301-72
specification, wire for circumferential reinforcement was required to conform to ASTM A227 except
wires with higher tensile strengths than those listed in A227 could be used if they met the other
requirements of A227.  ASTM A227-71 listed Class I and Class II options for both 8-gage (0.162-inch)
and 6-gage (0.192-inch) diameter wires.
8-gage Class I wire had a minimum tensile strength of 200,000 psi and a maximum tensile strength of
230,000 psi. Class II wire had a minimum tensile strength of 231,000 psi and a maximum tensile
strength of 261,000 psi.
6-gage Class I wire had a minimum tensile strength of 192,000 psi and a maximum tensile strength of
221,000 psi. Class II wire had a minimum tensile strength of 222,000 psi and a maximum tensile
strength of 251,000 psi.
AWWA c301-72 required steel for cylinders to meet the requirements of the ASTM A415
(Specification for Hot-Rolled Carbon Steel Sheets, Commercial Quality), except that the maximum
carbon content may be 0.25 per cent and the minimum yield point shall be 27,000 psi.

TORSION TEST METHOD AND RESULTS
There never were torsion test requirements for prestressing wire in ASTM A227.  Additionally, none
were incorporated into ASTM A648 until the 1988 revisions, and this same revision eliminated 8-gage
wire.  However, the torsion test is an excellent method for identifying wire that has been drawn at
excessive temperatures subjecting it to the effects of dynamic strain aging (DSA).
Torsion tests are performed by RJPA according to the contemporary test method specified in ASTM
A938-18, Standard Method for Torsion Testing of Wire.  In addition to the basic requirements in the
standard, RJPA conducts the torsion test on a modified machine lathe incorporating a data acquisition
system for continuous recording of revolutions and torque.
As allowed in Section 6.3 (End Preparation) of ASTM A938-18, the ends of the torsion specimen are
bent approximately 90Â° to the axis of the test specimen to prevent the gripped ends from slipping
during the test.
Torsion test results are listed in Table 4 and the torque-versus-rotation graphs for these same tests are
plotted in Figure 19 (wires from the failed pipe segment) and Figure 24 (wires from two additional
segments).  Photos documenting the torsion failures of the 8-gage wires from the failed pipe segment
are shown in Figure 15 through Figure 18.  Photos documenting the torsion failures of the 8-gage wires
from Pipe 20095 are shown in Figure 20 and Figure 21.  Photos documenting the torsion failures of the
6-gage wires from Pipe 20219 are shown in Figure 22 and Figure 23.
Torsion Results of 8-Gage Wires from Failed Pipe
From Table 4, all four wires from the failed pipe segment had poor torsional performance, failing in a
splitty manner between 0.49 and 7.97 revolutions.  Therefore, this wire would not meet the current 10-
revolution torsional requirement without any splitting failures for ASTM A648 6-gage wire, and the
torsion test confirms that the wire was likely subjected to DSA at the time of manufacture.
From Table 4, the maximum torque for Specimen 1556 (Good Wire 2) is significantly higher than the
maximum torque for the other three wires from the failed pipe segment.  The reason for this is because
the other three wires had pre-existing longitudinal splits prior to testing, as evidenced by the oxidized
surface of the split that became evident while testing (refer to Figure 15, Figure 17 and Figure 18), but
Specimen 1556 did not contain a pre-existing split.  Instead, Specimen 1556 split during the torsion
test, after approximately 0.7 revolutions.  This longitudinal splitting is further evidence of excessive
heating of the wire during the manufacturing process, which can result in excessive residual tensile
stresses upon cooling.

HYDROGEN EMBRITTLEMENT (HE) SENSITIVITY TEST METHOD AND RESULTS
The European Federation Internationale de la Precontrainte (FIP) developed a laboratory test during
the late 1960's and early 1970's for evaluation of the hydrogen embrittlement (HE) sensitivity of PCCP
prestressing wire. A recommended procedure for performing the test was published by the organization
in 1980 that is most often referred to as the FIP ammonium thiocyanate test. Lewis Engineering and
Consulting, Inc. (LEC) constructed a dedicated test system in 1990 for performing the FIP test in
accordance with the recommended procedure in support of forensic investigations of PCCP failures.
Based upon test data acquired by LEC between 1990 and 2002 on prestressing wire manufactured
between the mid-1950's to 2002, a presentation was made by LEC to the ASTM A01.05 Wire Products
Task Group subcommittee proposing the development of an ASTM standard for the test.  The result
was the ASTM A1032-04 test procedure published by ASTM in September 2004, Standard Test
Method for Hydrogen Embrittlement Resistance for Steel Wire Hard Drawn Used for Prestressing
Concrete Pipe. Supplementary Requirements were added concurrently to ASTM A648-04a addressing
the use of A1032-04 in purchase order specifications for contemporary pipe wrap wire. RJPA
purchased the HE tests system from LEC in November 2019 and now conducts HE tests in accordance
with the latest revision of the standard, A1032-15 (Reapproved 2019).
A 16-inch-long Specimen of hand-straightened wire is prepared for the HE test. The wire straightened
specimens are fitted and sealed into a Pyrex T-shaped test cell that will be filled with a 20 percent (by
weight) ammonium thiocyanate solution. The wire specimens are loaded in tension at a force
equivalent to 70% of the specified minimum tensile strength. A resistance heating coil wrapped around
the outside of the test cell and connected to a proportioning temperature controller is employed to heat
the solution within the cell to 50 C and maintain that temperature for the duration of the test. A switch
activated relay stops the timer upon failure and records the time-to-failure (TTF) to the nearest 0.01
hours. Test Specimens are removed at failure, or after 100 hours if no failure occurs.
Based upon the extensive experience with the HE sensitivity test method by Richard Lewis at LEC,
wire that exhibits practically no HE sensitivity will consistently survive the test for 75 hours or more.
An increasing degree of HE sensitivity is indicated by failure times progressively less than 75 hours. A
slight-to-moderate HE sensitivity is indicated by a time-to-failure (TTF) of approximately 50 hours,
and a significant HE sensitivity is indicated for TTF less than 20 hours.

Failed Pipe
The tensile strength of the two 8-gage prestressing wires that were labeled

Wires from the broken pipe also had very poor torsional ductility.  Three of the torsion specimens had
a pre-existing longitudinal split and a fourth torsion specimen developed a longitudinal split with less
than one full revolution.  This indicates that the wire was likely drawn at a high temperature resulting
in dynamic strain aging (DSA).  The prevalence of longitudinal splitting is also consistent with high
drawing temperatures.
HE exposure testing of four wires from this same pipe revealed that the wire had a significant HE
sensitivity, with an average TTF of 16.16 hours.  Furthermore, three of the four wires had a TTF of
less than 7 hours.
A tensile test of the steel cylinder indicated that the steel still meets the minimum yield strength
requirement that was in place at the time of manufacture.
Therefore, the 8-gage wire samples provided by Pure from the failed pipe segment are in overall poor
condition, and it is likely that hydrogen embrittlement of the prestressing wires contributed to the
failure of the pipe.
Pipe 20095
The tensile strength of the two 8-gage prestressing wires from Pipe 20095 averaged 275.2 ksi, making
the wire a Class III wire according to ASTM A648-72.  The two wires also had excellent ductility with
a RA averaging 46.0%.
Wires from Pipe 20095 had very poor torsional ductility.  The two torsion specimens had a pre-
existing longitudinal split but were still able to rotate more than 11 revolutions prior to failing in shear.
This presence of the pre-existing longitudinal split indicates that the wire was likely drawn at a high
temperature resulting in dynamic strain aging (DSA).
HE exposure testing of two wires from this same pipe revealed that the wire had a significant HE
sensitivity, with an average TTF of 3.51 hours.

dynamic strain aging (DSA).

These excellent HE test results are remarkable, especially since the wire had poor torsional
performance as exhibited by longitudinal splitting of the wire at less than one revolution during the
torsion test.  The reason for this apparent inconsistency is unknown.

that the cylinder met the minimum tensile requirement at the time of manufacture and that a sufficient
weld was achieved.
