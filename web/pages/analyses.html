4 Pump Operations Summary

4
PUMP OPERATIONS SUMMARY
AE conducted a pump operations review immediately following the rupture of the BSFM; the technical memorandum
is included in Appendix A. This review included an examination of pump operations prior to the failure, as well as a
hydraulic analysis of the BSFM during a simulated transient pressure event. According to this review, pump operations
immediately before the failure did not contribute to the failure. The modeled pressures during the simulated transient
event were within bounds and not a contributing factor.

5 Finite Element Analysis & Limit State Design

5
FINITE ELEMENT ANALYSIS & LIMIT STATE DESIGN
Pure was enlisted by The City to develop Performance Curves through Finite Element Analysis (FEA) to determine
when a pipe will reach various limit states as defined by AWWA C304, based on internal pressure, depth of cover, and
the number of wire breaks per pipe segment. The Performance Curves for the Class 16 pipe at two depths of cover
are provided in Figures 5-1 and 5-2. At the time of writing this report, AE had not received Performance Curves for
the Class 20 pipe.

Pure’s Performance Curves for the Class 16 1950 mm pipe show that the normal working pressure and design
pressure (accounting for potential transients/surge) are well within the yield strength and ultimate strength of the
pipe. However, the Performance Curves show that Class 16 pipes could possibly experience microcracking of the
external mortar due to mild transient events, especially in areas with deep cover (significant external loading) or if any
prestress wires have already been compromised. Microcracks are very small cracks within the mortar that are invisible
to the naked eye, and an indication that the pipe has experienced significant internal or external stresses.

Microcracking has no immediate impact on the structural integrity of a pipe but compromises the ability of the mortar
to protect the prestressed wires from moisture and interaction with the soil around the pipe. There is a possibility that
surge pressures resulted in microcracking of some pipes due to operations over the last 50 years. While Performance
Curves for Class 20 pipes and 1500 mm pipes have not been provided, it is possible that the working pressures and
external loading in those areas, combined with transient events, resulted in microcracking of those pipes as well.

AWWA C304, which requires pipes to be designed according to the limit states as shown in Pure’s Performance
Curves, was introduced in 1992. AWWA C304 introduced a unified design procedure that replaced two procedures
previously outlined in AWWA C301-84. Therefore, it is possible that the design of the pipe did not account for
microcracking while still meeting applicable standards at that time.

7 Stray Current Assessment

7
STRAY CURRENT ASSESSMENT
Corrpro was retained by AE to conduct tests for stray current and the presence of cathodic protection on the pipeline
at the initial rupture location, as well as the subsequent Phase 1 repair sites. Their report is included in Appendix C
with key findings as follows:
•
Corrpro conducted pipe to soil DC potential measurements that confirmed no cathodic protection exists on
the pipeline as per City records.
•
The DC potential was -302 mV, less than the -1000 mV industry threshold of causing hydrogen
embrittlement.
•
No significant levels of stray current were found.
•
Low AC potential indicate unlikely AC influence being a factor.
•
Corrpro noted that while the voltage potential itself would not likely be a concern, damaged mortar coating
could be a contributing factor to steel corrosion, and stray current pickup or discharge in those areas cannot
be ruled out.

would be subject to accelerated corrosion and failure rates.

While AWWA M9 did not exist at the time of manufacture or installation, the current edition of M9 specifically states
that supplemental protection of C301 pipe is required in buried applications with high chloride environments. It goes
on to specify that in areas where resistivities are below 1500 ohm-cm (common in Calgary), and water-soluble chloride
exceeds 400 ppm (400 mg/L), one of several specific mitigation steps need to be taken in either the manufacture or
construction of the feedermain. Based on available information, none of the mitigative steps outlined in M9 were
applied to the feedermain, nor were they expected to be for a pipe of this vintage.

A desktop review of the soil sampling data in relation to the electromagnetic scan information shown on Figure 6-1,
through the use of GIS software, revealed the following general observations:
•
Comparing the wire break data in Section 6 to the chloride and resistivity data along the entirety of the ~9 km

•
There are some moderate to severe distressed pipes being reported near areas with relatively low chloride
and low sulphates levels. Fluctuating groundwater levels can often deposit high levels of chlorides and
sulphates on pipes in soils that have relatively low chloride or sulphate concentrations.
•
There are areas with high levels of chlorides that do not correlate with severe areas of pipe distress.
•
Soil samples showing both moderate and significant chloride levels are adjacent to pipes with moderate
distress, even though some resistivity values are high and are therefore considered to have low corrosivity
soil. This could indicate that these areas have fluctuating groundwater levels that deposit the chlorides on the

•
In 2014, some areas along the feedermain exhibited moderate to severe levels of chlorides

8.2
Geo-Environmental Assessment
AE conducted a Geo-Environmental review along the feedermain which indicated a moderate to high potential for soil
and groundwater contamination due to current or past activities. Refer to Appendix F. Specifically, road de-icing
activities using sodium chloride is highly likely to have contributed to the elevated chloride concentrations found in
the soil samples. While The City has confirmed that both sodium chloride (road salt) and liquid calcium chloride (brine)
have been used as de-icers in the past, AE discovered a strong correlation between the elevated sodium and chloride
concentrations.

Furthermore, AE’s Geo-Environmental review concluded that high concentrations of chlorides in greenspaces are not
surprising. This is because chlorides from meltwater can migrate and accumulate in these areas, due to multiple factors
including the presence of gravel soils common in the region.

9 Metallurgical Analysis

9
METALLURGICAL ANALYSIS
Pure obtained samples of the prestress wire and steel cylinder of the ruptured and exhumed pipes for analysis. Their
report is provided in Appendix G. In summary, the lab analysis revealed the following information:
•
The steel cylinder and wires met AWWA C301 requirements for strength at the time of manufacturing.
•
The prestress wires exhibited poor torsional ductility; however, this was not a requirement of AWWA
standards at the time.
•
Some of the wires sent for analysis had longitudinal splits, consistent with wires drawn at high temperatures
resulting in Dynamic Strain Aging.
•
The 8-gauge wire used on Class 16 pipe had HE sensitivity.
•
The 6-gauge wire used on Class 20 pipe had no sensitivity to HE.

10 Mortar and Concrete Analysis

10
MORTAR AND CONCRETE ANALYSIS
Tetra Tech was retained by Pure to conduct laboratory analysis of the mortar and concrete core samples obtained
from the June 5, 2024 rupture. Tetra Tech’s report (in Appendix H) concluded that the mortar and concrete used in
the Class 16 pipe, which ruptured, was of good to excellent quality and would have met C301 standards. There was no
mortar deterioration or chemical attack, and the level of chlorides in the mortar and concrete were low, so they were
not a contributing factor to the deterioration of the steel components.

AE’s subconsultant Thurber Engineering conducted an analysis of the mortar and concrete samples of the pipes which
were exhumed in the Phase 1 repairs. Thurber’s analysis (in Appendix D) determined that 3 of the 4 pipes exhumed
(Pure Robotic Pipe 20095, 20202, 20219) had chloride ions penetrate the mortar that exceeded American Concrete
Institute thresholds.
•
Pipe 20095 showed evidence of chloride penetration in the mortar samples where distress was noted, but not
in areas where the pipe was in otherwise good shape. There was also no chloride penetration of the outer
concrete core.
•
Pipe 20202 showed evidence of chloride penetration in the mortar samples where distress was noted, but not
in areas where the pipe was in otherwise good shape. There was evidence of chloride penetration of the outer
concrete core in some of the distressed areas.
•
Pipe 20219 showed evidence of chloride penetration in the mortar samples where distress was noted, and
generally not where the pipe appeared sound.
•
Pipe 20220 had only one core taken, which did not show evidence of chloride penetration of the mortar or
concrete.

It is important to note here that the fabrication drawings of the pipes routinely listed the use of Type V Sulphate
resistant cement. While soils with high sulphates are more commonly found in Calgary than soils with high chloride
levels, sulphate resistant cement is considered to be one of the least resistant mixes to chloride penetration.

11 Live Load Assessment

11
LIVE LOAD ASSESSMENT
Visual examination of the exhumed pipes in the Phase 1 repairs did not reveal signs of visual cracking consistent with
overloading or other stresses. While microcracking due to live loads cannot be ruled out on the ruptured and exhumed
pipe, the distribution of distressed pipes within green space, medians, and residential roads is relatively similar to those
under major roads. Therefore, live loading subsequent to construction is not believed to be a factor, however
microcracking due to live loads during original construction activities such as backfill, remains a possibility.
