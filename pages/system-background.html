1 Introduction

1
INTRODUCTION
1.1
Scope of Work
The purpose of this report is to provide The City of Calgary (The City) with a summary of the forensic investigation
into the failure of The City’s Bearspaw South Feedermain (BSFM), and distressed pipe located subsequent to the initial
failure. This report will outline the findings obtained through various field, laboratory, and desktop investigations of
the initial rupture and subsequent Phase 1 repairs. This report will not detail any findings of the Phase 2 repairs, as
they were ongoing at the time of this report but will cover the electromagnetic inspection results of the entire
Prestressed Concrete Cylinder Pipe (AWWA C301) portion of the BSFM. Key findings of potential causes for the
material failures and pipe distress will be provided, but this report is not intended to be a root cause analysis.

1.2
Background
On June 5, 2024, The City experienced a significant break on the BSFM. This feedermain is the backbone of The City’s
transmission network, conveying water from the Bearspaw Water Treatment Plant (BWTP) directly into the Glenmore
Pressure Zone (PZ). This is The City’s largest PZ, which feeds numerous other PZ’s, ultimately providing water service
to over 90% of The City’s population, as well as Regional Customers. See Figure 1-1 on the following page.

The BSFM is 10.84 km in length and is comprised of the following segments: 1.61 km 1950 mm diameter AWWA C200 steel pipe. 5.94 km 1950 mm diameter AWWA C301 prestressed concrete cylinder pipe (PCCP). 3.29 km 1500 mm diameter AWWA C301 PCCP.

During the response to the initial rupture, The City performed electromagnetic inspections of the pipe 2.5 km
upstream (west) and 1.7 km downstream (east) of the failed pipe. This was the maximum length of pipe that could be
inspected given equipment and time limitations. These inspections revealed additional distressed pipe segments
resulting in the decision to perform immediate repairs of five additional sites before returning the BSFM to service.
Following return to service, The City performed further electromagnetic inspection of the BSFM, covering the full
5.9 km length of 1950 mm diameter AWWA C301 and 3.3 km of 1500 mm diameter AWWA C301 pipe that forms
part of the BSFM. An additional 0.5 km of 1200 mm C301 feedermain leading into the Memorial Drive Pump
Station #5 was inspected as well. The steel (AWWA C200) section of BSFM was not inspected.

The City requested Associated Engineering (AE) to conduct a forensic investigation of the initial rupture, also
considering information gathered from the proactively repaired pipes in Phase 1 which were identified through
electromagnetic inspection.

2 Bearspaw South Feedermain

2
BEARSPAW SOUTH FEEDERMAIN
2.1
Description
Construction of the 1950 mm and 1500 mm portions of the BSFM were completed in 1975. The portion of pipe that
is the focus of this report is AWWA C-301 (ECP), commonly referred to as Prestressed Concrete Cylinder Pipe (PCCP)
Embedded Cylinder Type.
The pipe was fabricated by Canron, which eventually became Hyprescon, then Hanson, then Forterra, and is now
known as Rinker. This type of pipe contains a thin gauge steel cylinder embedded within a concrete core. The pipe is
then laid horizontally and spun while prestressing wires are wound around it under stresses exceeding 150,000 psi.
The wires are then coated in a mortar mix. It is primarily the prestress wire diameter (gauge) and spacing (pitch) that
provide the internal pressure capacity of the pipe, while the steel cylinder acts primarily as a water barrier and not a
structural element. See Figure 2-1 below.

Figure 2-1 Cross Section of Typical C-301-ECP pipe per AWWA M9

Unique to this pipe is that the pressure classes, steel gauges, wire pitch, mortar and concrete core thickness, joints,
and lay lengths are generally dictated by the manufacturer based on project design requirements, in accordance with
AWWA design criteria. Pipe joints typically consist of bell and spigot made up of steel joint rings and rubber gaskets.
These joints are then protected by mortar-based field coatings commonly referred to as diapers.

Catastrophic failures of C301 pipe are not unusual given the nature of its design. While non-catastrophic leaks do
occur at joints, non-catastrophic leaks in the barrel section are considered rare. Catastrophic failures typically occur in
the barrel section. Across North America, the primary causes for the pipe to fail catastrophically in the barrel section
include:
•
Hydrogen embrittlement of the prestress wires, due to either stray current or the use of prestress wire with
hydrogen embrittlement sensitivity.
•
Sulphate attack of the mortar coating, allowing corrosion of the steel wires and cylinder to occur.
•
Chlorides penetrating the mortar/concrete and attacking the steel wires and cylinder.
•
Installation damage or poor-quality installation.
•
Third party damage after installation.

2.3
Pressure Rating
The 1950 mm portion of the BSFM conveys water from the Bearspaw WTP High Lift Pump Station (HLPS) at an
elevation of 1083 m. Portions of the feedermain drop to as low as 1053 m based on the plan and profile record
drawings. It is AE’s understanding that the maximum pump head at the HLPS is approximately 57 m (90 psi). Based on
The City’s Feedermain Specifications, the conservative working pressure of the feedermain at the lowest point along
the 1950 mm portion is then 869 kPa (126 psi). The design pressure (which considers a transient event) for the lowest
portion of the feedermain would be 1310 kPa (190 psi) based on The City’s current standards, and 1145 kPa (166 psi)
based on current AWWA C304 standard. The City’s current design pressure requirements exceed current
AWWA C304 requirements which only require 40 psi surge pressure to be added to the working pressure. It is AE’s
understanding that The City’s current standards for calculating pressure ratings were common practice at The City in
the 1970’s.

The Canron fabrication drawings in The City’s records are missing a key page showing key design data, and do not
state the design pressure of the various pressure classes used along the pipe. Rinker indicated they do not have the
manufacturing records or design calculations of the pipe and were therefore unable to confirm the pressure ratings of
the pipe used on the project. Rinker referred AE to the AWWA C304 standard to determine each individual pipe
segment’s pressure rating. It is noted that C304 did not exist at the time of pipe manufacture, as the sole standard of
the day was C301.

The 1950 mm C301 portion of the BSFM is comprised of Canron Class 16 and Class 20 pipe segments. Past versions
of the manufacturer’s catalogues list the pressure classes of C301 pipe at various diameters and state the standard
working pressure ratings at various depths of cover via design charts. In general, these catalogues listed C301 Class 16
pipe as 1103 kPa (160 psi) working pressure at 0 m cover, and Class 20 at 1379 kPa (200 psi) working pressure at 0 m
cover for various pipe diameters. These pressure ratings decreased as the depth of cover increased. AE could not
source a catalogue specifically showing the 1950 mm diameter of C301 (ECP). As well, Rinker did not want to make
the assumption that the pipe used for the BSFM had the same ratings.

The original pipe that ruptured was a Class 16 pipe. Located at Sta. 136+74 to 136+90 ft, its invert was at an elevation
of approximately 1060 m. This equates to a working pressure of 800 kPa (116 psi), and a design pressure of 1200 kPa
(174 psi) based on current City standards, and a design pressure of 1076 kPa (156 psi) per C304. The City’s record
drawings provide evidence in numerous places that the Class 16 pipe was rated for a working pressure of at least
150 psi. While unconfirmed at this diameter, as noted above, Canron Class 16 C301 pipe was typically rated for
160 psi working pressure at 0 m cover.

Given the information available, there is no indication that the pipe’s original pressure ratings were not appropriate,
given the standards at the time. This is further supported by Performance Curves generated for The City by Pure,
which are discussed later in this report. This assumes the various pipe classes were installed at the appropriate
locations as per the lay sheets. There are no indications based on the exhumed pipe that the various pipe classes were
installed at incorrect locations, however Pure’s electromagnetic scans indicate changes were made during pipe
fabrication and installation that were not captured on the record drawings.

2.4
AWWA Standards
There are currently three AWWA documents applicable to the design, manufacture, and installation of prestressed
concrete cylinder pipe:
•
AWWA C301 Standard for Prestressed Concrete Cylinder Pipe.
•
AWWA C304 Standard for Design of Prestressed Concrete Cylinder Pipe.
•
AWWA M9 Design Manual for Concrete Pressure Pipe.

At the time this pipe was manufactured in 1974, only the C301 standard was in existence, having been first introduced
in 1949. AWWA C304 was not introduced until 1992, and the M9 manual was first introduced in 1979. This is an
important distinction to make as key changes were made to the design of PCCP pipe after 1979 which will be
discussed further in this report.

The American Concrete Pressure Pipe Association (ACPPA) was established in 1949 (the same year as C301) and
represents most North American PCCP manufacturers. The ACPPA generally references AWWA standards.
